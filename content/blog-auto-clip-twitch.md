## 5 Tools for Auto-Clipping Twitch Streams (Compared)

I've tested the main options. Here's where they actually stand.

### 1. Eklipse

Eklipse was one of the first AI clipping tools to gain traction with streamers. It connects to your Twitch VODs and uses AI to detect kill moments, funny reactions, and high-energy segments.

**What it does well:** The detection is decent for FPS games. It picks up multi-kills and team wipes with reasonable accuracy. The interface is clean and straightforward.

**Where it falls short:** It doesn't post for you. You still have to download clips and manually upload to each platform. The AI also struggles with non-shooter games. If you play RPGs, horror games, or anything narrative-driven, the detection misses a lot. Free tier gives you limited clips per month before you're paying $15-20/month for more.

**Verdict:** Good detection, but you're still doing the posting yourself.

### 2. Opus Clip

Opus Clip got famous for repurposing long-form YouTube videos into shorts. It's not built specifically for live streams, but some streamers use it on their VODs after the fact.

**What it does well:** The AI scoring system is genuinely useful. It rates clips by virality potential, and those scores are surprisingly accurate. Caption generation is solid out of the box.

**Where it falls short:** It wasn't designed for gaming streams. It's optimized for talking-head content, podcasts, and educational videos. Feed it a Valorant VOD and it'll find moments where you're talking, not moments where you're fragging. Pricing starts around $19/month but scales up fast if you're processing multiple long VODs per week.

**Verdict:** Great for podcasters. Not great for gamers.

### 3. Medal.tv

Medal is more of a clip capture tool than an AI editor. You set hotkeys, clip the last 15-60 seconds of gameplay, and share them on Medal's social platform.

**What it does well:** The capture quality is excellent. Low performance impact on your system. The community features are actually fun if you're into browsing other people's clips.

**Where it falls short:** It's manual. You have to press a button to clip. There's no AI detection, no auto-posting to TikTok, and no editing pipeline. It also pushes hard toward keeping your content on Medal's own platform rather than distributing it outward.

**Verdict:** Good capture tool. Not an auto-clipper.

### 4. Clipbot

Clipbot positions itself as an AI stream clipper that handles detection and basic editing. It supports Twitch and some YouTube integration.

**What it does well:** The setup is fast. Connect your Twitch account, pick your game, and it starts processing. Basic highlight detection works for popular titles.

**Where it falls short:** Limited game support. If you're playing anything outside the top 20 most-streamed games, accuracy drops hard. No direct posting to social platforms. You download and upload manually.

**Verdict:** Decent starter tool, but gaps in automation.
